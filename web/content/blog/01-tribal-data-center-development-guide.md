The hyperscalers are not waiting. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta collectively committed over $300 billion in capital expenditure in 2024 and 2025, most of it pointed at data center expansion. The AI training runs that power large language models require power at a scale that has effectively exhausted available industrial land in the established markets — Northern Virginia, Phoenix, Dallas, Chicago. Site selectors are now hunting in markets they would have dismissed five years ago, and they are finding that some of the most viable parcels in North America sit on land held by tribal nations.

This is not a coincidence. Tribal land often offers the combination of contiguous acreage, proximity to transmission infrastructure, and development flexibility that commercial developers cannot find anywhere else at comparable scale. The AI boom did not create this advantage — it revealed it. The question now is whether tribal nations and their Indigenous Development Corporations will participate in this wave as principals with equity, revenue, and governance rights, or as passive landlords collecting ground rent while someone else captures the economic value.

The window to answer that question is not open indefinitely. Data center developers work on 18-to-36-month site selection cycles. Nations that enter the conversation now, with a clear sense of their assets and negotiating position, can shape deal terms. Nations that wait until a developer has already identified their land and arrived with a term sheet will be negotiating from a much weaker position. The difference between those two scenarios is preparation, not luck.

This guide is written for tribal councils, IDC boards, and their advisors. It covers what your land actually offers, what the federal funding landscape does and does not cover, how the IDC structure works and why developers often misunderstand it, the questions you need to answer before any developer walks in the door, and what a deal structure that preserves sovereignty actually looks like.

## What Tribal Land Actually Offers Data Center Developers

Site selectors evaluating data center locations score against a short list of hard constraints: power, land, water (for cooling), fiber connectivity, and permitting timeline. Tribal land frequently scores well on all five, and exceptionally well on the last two.

**Power access and interconnect rights.** Many tribal territories in the western United States and Canada sit adjacent to or beneath existing transmission corridors. Tribes that hold land near hydroelectric assets, high-voltage transmission lines, or generation facilities have a structural advantage. More importantly, tribal governments can negotiate directly with utilities and, in some cases, develop their own generation assets without navigating state public utility commission processes. In states where PUC approval adds 18 to 36 months to a power agreement, the ability to move on a tribal timeline is worth real money to a developer.

**Contiguous acreage at scale.** Modern hyperscale data center campuses require 200 to 500 acres of developable land, often in a single contiguous parcel, with room for expansion. Commercial land of this size near existing transmission infrastructure in the continental United States is effectively unavailable at reasonable prices. Tribal land in the Pacific Northwest, the Plains, and across Canada's Prairie provinces offers exactly this at scale. A developer who would otherwise spend two to three years assembling parcels from multiple private owners can often solve their land problem in a single negotiation with one sovereign counterparty.

**Permitting under tribal sovereignty.** This is the most significant and least understood advantage. Tribal lands held in trust are not subject to state or county zoning, environmental review under state law, or local building codes. A data center development on tribal land bypasses the state PUC processes, county zoning boards, and CEQA or SEPA reviews that can add years to a project timeline in California, Washington, or Oregon. The tribe establishes its own development standards and administers its own permitting process. For a developer trying to bring a campus online before a hyperscaler's capacity deadline, the ability to move on a sovereign permitting timeline rather than a California county timeline is not a minor consideration — it can be the deciding factor.

**Rural fiber paths.** The NTIA and USDA broadband programs of the last five years have funded significant fiber deployment in rural areas, much of it through tribal and adjacent networks. Nations that have completed or are completing broadband infrastructure now sit on fiber routes that connect to regional and national backbone networks. Data center operators need diverse, redundant fiber paths. Tribal networks increasingly provide exactly that.

**Water and cooling access.** In the Pacific Northwest and parts of Canada, tribal lands adjacent to rivers and lakes offer cooling water access that is becoming genuinely scarce in the water-stressed Southwest. This is not universal — nations in arid regions face real constraints — but where it applies, it represents a meaningful competitive advantage over sites in Arizona or Nevada.

## The Federal Funding Landscape — What It Covers and What It Doesn't

The federal investment in tribal infrastructure over the last four years is real and substantial, but it is also widely misunderstood, including by some of the developers who approach tribal nations as potential partners.

The NTIA Tribal Broadband Connectivity Program (TBCP) has allocated roughly $3 billion in grants to tribal broadband projects since its creation under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. USDA ReConnect has funded additional rural broadband deployment, with tribal applicants receiving a significant share of awards. The DOE Loan Programs Office has financing authority that extends to clean energy and transmission projects with tribal involvement. In Canada, Indigenous Services Canada and the Universal Broadband Fund have funded First Nations connectivity projects, and provincial Indigenous loan guarantee programs in Alberta, Ontario, and British Columbia have provided structured financing for energy and infrastructure projects.

These programs are building the foundational layer — the fiber, the power interconnects, the middle-mile networks that make tribal land viable as data center locations. That investment is real and it matters.

What it does not cover is the enterprise connectivity layer and the data center operations that run on top of the infrastructure. NTIA grants build the road; they do not fund the logistics company that moves freight on it. A tribal nation that completes a TBCP-funded fiber deployment has an asset — but monetizing that asset through commercial carrier agreements, data center anchor tenancy, and enterprise connectivity contracts requires a different set of capabilities and a different set of counterparties.

This is the gap that Konative fills. The federal programs create the infrastructure foundation. The commercial opportunity on top of that foundation requires vendor-neutral advisory that connects tribal assets to the developer, carrier, and enterprise buyer markets. Understanding which federal programs apply to your current project phase, and where the handoff from grant-funded infrastructure to commercially structured operations begins, is one of the first things any serious readiness process has to establish.

One other point on federal funding: grants and loans are not substitutes for equity. A nation that finances data center infrastructure through federal grants and then leases that infrastructure to a developer at a ground lease rate has traded an equity position for a fixed income stream. That may be the right structure in some circumstances. But it should be a deliberate choice, not the default outcome because no one at the table modeled the alternative.

## Understanding the IDC Structure and Why It Matters

The Indigenous Development Corporation is the vehicle through which most tribal commercial development activity flows, and it is also the most consistently misunderstood element of tribal data center transactions — misunderstood by developers, by lenders, and sometimes by tribal councils themselves.

An IDC is a tribally chartered commercial entity, typically structured as a corporation or LLC, that is owned by the tribal government but operates at arm's length from it. The IDC holds commercial contracts, employs staff, takes on debt, and enters into joint ventures. The critical feature is that the IDC's commercial liabilities do not automatically become the tribe's liabilities. This separation protects the tribal government's sovereign assets — land, trust funds, governmental programs — from the commercial risks that come with infrastructure development and operations.

For lenders and developers, the IDC structure creates a familiar counterparty: a corporate entity with a board, audited financials, and the ability to pledge assets and sign contracts. The tribal government's sovereign immunity is a feature of the relationship — it clarifies what the counterparty actually is — not an obstacle. Lenders who understand the structure can work with it. Lenders who do not understand it often demand tribal council guarantees or sovereign immunity waivers that no council should provide for a commercial transaction.

The governance requirements of IDC structures vary significantly by nation. Some IDCs have full commercial authority delegated by council resolution. Others require council approval for commitments above a specified threshold. Understanding the specific governance requirements of the IDC you are working with — what it can commit to, what requires council sign-off, what the approval timeline looks like — is not optional. It is foundational due diligence. Developers who skip this step often find themselves three months into a negotiation with a term sheet that cannot be executed because the IDC board lacked authority to commit to it.

## The Five Questions Every Council Should Be Able to Answer Before a Developer Shows Up

You will not have weeks to prepare once a serious developer identifies your land. The preparation happens before the conversation starts. These are the five questions your council or IDC board needs to be able to answer before any substantive meeting with a developer or investor.

**1. Do we have an interconnection study?**
Developers need to know your power capacity and the timeline to bring additional capacity online. An interconnection study from your utility tells both of you what the realistic ceiling is. Without it, any power commitment you make is speculative and any developer will discount it accordingly.

**2. What is our power ceiling, and what does expansion cost?**
Related to the interconnection study but distinct: what is the maximum load you can serve today, what would it cost to expand that ceiling, and who bears that cost in a potential deal? Data centers are power-intensive. A 100MW campus requires answers to these questions before site selection is complete.

**3. Is the land in trust or fee simple, and what are the BIA or INAC/ISC approvals required?**
Trust land and fee simple land have different development processes. A 25-year ground lease on trust land requires BIA approval in the United States. Understanding the approval pathway and timeline before you represent a site to a developer is basic due diligence. Surprises here kill deals.

**4. What does your IDC governance require for a commitment of this size?**
Does your IDC board have authority to sign a development agreement? What threshold triggers council approval? What is the realistic timeline from term sheet to signed agreement given your governance structure? These answers need to be documented, not just understood verbally.

**5. Do you have a neutral advisor who is not also a developer?**
This is the question most councils fail to ask early enough. Many of the firms that approach tribal nations as "advisors" on data center development also hold developer interests, equity positions, or referral arrangements with specific vendors. Their advice is not neutral. A nation entering a significant infrastructure transaction needs representation whose fee structure does not depend on which developer wins the project.

## What a Sovereignty-Preserving Deal Structure Looks Like

Most term sheets that commercial data center developers bring to tribal counterparties are designed for commercial landowners. They are not designed for sovereign nations, and the default terms reflect that. Understanding the structural options — and the implications of each — is the most important thing an IDC board or council advisor can do before entering negotiations.

**Ground lease** is the simplest structure: the nation leases land to a developer for a fixed term at a negotiated rate. The developer builds and operates. The nation collects rent. This is not necessarily wrong, but it is the structure that captures the least value for the nation. Ground lease rates for industrial land, even attractive industrial land, are a fraction of the economic value generated by the data center operation. If your land is in a power-constrained market where developers are competing for sites, a ground lease is leaving significant money on the table.

**Joint venture** structures give the nation an equity stake in the development entity. The nation contributes land, potentially power access and permitting advantages, in exchange for an ownership percentage. This captures more value — revenue participation, upside on sale or refinancing — but requires the nation to accept more complexity in governance, financial reporting, and ongoing operations. The IDC structure exists precisely to hold positions like this without exposing tribal government assets.

**Development agreement** is the structure that gives nations the most control. The nation, through its IDC, directs the development process: selecting contractors, approving designs, controlling the permitting timeline, and choosing the end tenant. Third-party developers provide capital, expertise, and relationships but do not control the project. This structure requires the most capacity from the IDC but preserves the most sovereignty and captures the most long-term value.

The specific term to watch in any developer's initial term sheet is the exclusivity clause. Developers routinely ask for 12-to-24-month exclusivity periods during which the nation cannot discuss the land with other parties. That clause exists to protect the developer's site control while they pursue financing. It does not protect the nation. If you sign it without understanding what you are giving up, you have effectively handed the developer all the negotiating leverage for the duration of the exclusivity period. Any exclusivity provision in a deal of this significance should be reviewed by counsel with specific tribal commercial real estate experience before execution.
