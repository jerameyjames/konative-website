## Problem Statement
Konative is entering a supply-constrained market where land and capital are available, but traditional data center development is too slow and fragmented to convert that demand into credible shovel-ready plans. The immediate problem is not "how do we build a big platform," but "how do we give six live prospects a high-trust destination that makes Konative feel real, capable, and worth engaging now?"

The website needs to help close active opportunities in a market where waiting costs months. It must explain the value clearly, show why Konative exists, and give serious buyers a clear next step without pretending the company is already a software platform.

The launch posture is **ASAP**. Demand exists now, so the public-facing trust surface should be shipped as soon as it is credible and safe to launch.

## Demand Evidence
- The founder said, "we have six companies asking us to do this work for them now."
- Those companies "are wanting us to put a pro forma in front of them."
- The urgency is not theoretical: "If someone doesn't make a commitment now, it pushes them out six to 12 months beyond their start time because of supply demands."
- First engagement economics are already legible: about `$300,000` per quarter, with a team generating `$200,000-$400,000` per quarter.
- Read as two useful lenses, not a contradiction: the `$300,000` figure is the likely client-facing engagement size, while the `$200,000-$400,000` range describes delivery-team revenue capacity across the work.

This is real pull, not abstract market enthusiasm.

## Status Quo
Today, prospects are living inside a three-part trap:

1. **Stuck waiting** on traditional data center timelines that are too slow for the current capital window.
2. **Piecing together consultants** across turbine sourcing, behind-the-meter gas, cooling, remote-site engineering, and construction.
3. **Paralyzed by complexity** because nobody is packaging the moving parts as one coherent program.

The founder described it directly: "there are too many moving parts that are complex, like turbine sourcing, behind-the-meter gas, and the engineering for remote areas that have to do with power consumption and cooling."

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge
### Target user
The best near-term buyer is not a generic "enterprise." It is a decision-maker inside:

- An **investment group** with capital that needs to be deployed into real infrastructure
- An **Indigenous Development Corporation** with land and development ambition, but no single technical quarterback

The actual human is likely a principal, managing partner, development lead, or executive sponsor whose job is to move from land + capital to a credible go/no-go plan before supply-chain and power windows close.

### Narrowest wedge
The smallest thing worth selling now is a **paid front-end engagement** that produces:

- feasibility and diligence across site, power, cooling, and supply chain
- a defensible pro forma
- a clear risk register and decision path
- a credible next-step recommendation toward out-of-ground execution

The site should sell that wedge first. It should not try to sell a broad future platform before the service offer is crisp.

## Constraints
- `konative.com` currently needs to serve **credibility first**, **lead generation second**, and **internal knowledge hub third**.
- Konative is currently an assumed business name under **Tolowa Pacific**, but the brand is intended to become a standalone LLC over time.
- This is still a **services-led business**, not a software product launch.
- The current site behavior includes redirect/automation quirks documented in `docs/requirements-prd.md`; avoid architecture assumptions that depend on brittle redirects.
- Confidentiality matters. Proof points may need to be anonymized until permissions are secured.
- The founder wants the site to become a knowledge base eventually, but that should not delay the public-facing trust surface needed now.

### Launch-critical behavior from the PRD
- The public root experience should become the **canonical trust entry point** if feasible, rather than forcing avoidable redirect hops.
- Any launch plan should preserve **fast mobile rendering**, a single primary CTA, and basic SEO/social metadata.
- Redirect behavior and any automation-sensitive paths should be validated in staging so public launch does not inherit the existing brittle behavior.
- The first release should avoid requiring authenticated internal-hub behavior on the main public path.
- PRD items that must survive into implementation: clear hero value prop, one primary CTA, working navigation/footer/legal links, accessible form behavior, SEO basics, and launch-safe redirect behavior.

## Premises

2. **The first paid offer should be a decision-grade diligence + pro forma engagement.**  
   Buyers need a go/no-go path, not a vague strategic study.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Credibility-First Site
**Summary:** Launch a high-trust public-facing site focused on positioning, offer clarity, urgency, and a direct CTA for the initial paid engagement.

**Effort:** S (`~1 sprint`)  
**Risk:** Low

**Pros**
- Fastest path to something useful for the six active opportunities
- Sharpest message discipline
- Avoids overbuilding internal systems before the offer is validated in market

**Cons**
- Internal knowledge-hub need remains mostly off-site for now
- Less differentiated on workflow than a client room or platform story
- Requires strong copy and proof architecture to carry trust

### Approach B: Public Site + Private Deal Room
**Summary:** Pair the public credibility site with a lightweight gated workspace for intake, artifacts, pro formas, and internal documents.

**Effort:** M (`~2-3 sprints`)  
**Risk:** Medium

**Pros**
- Better long-term bridge between marketing and actual deal execution
- Starts solving the internal repository problem early
- Creates a more distinctive client experience

**Cons**
- More product surface area than needed for the current urgency
- Higher design and implementation complexity
- Risks diffusing focus from the public trust story

### Approach C: Procurement-Trigger Product
**Summary:** Position Konative primarily around a decision-grade package that helps buyers reserve critical path items and move earlier on supply-chain commitments.

**Effort:** M (`~2-3 sprints`)  
**Risk:** Medium

**Pros**
- Strong alignment with the founder's "commit now or lose 6-12 months" insight
- Could become highly differentiated over time
- Moves the brand closer to a unique commercial product

**Cons**
- Requires clearer proof that buyers want procurement-adjacent value, not just diligence
- Harder to explain cleanly on a first-pass site without sounding speculative
- More likely to outrun what Konative can confidently operationalize today

## Recommended Approach
**Choose Approach A: Credibility-First Site.**

This is the right first move because Konative already has demand and needs a better **selling surface**, not a broader product thesis. The site should function like a serious introduction deck that closes the gap between "this sounds compelling in conversation" and "this is a real firm we should trust with a quarter-million-dollar engagement."

### What the site should communicate
1. **What Konative is**  
   A modular data center development partner that de-risks the path from land + capital to out-of-ground execution.
2. **Who it is for**  
   Investment groups and Indigenous Development Corps first; enterprise and adjacent buyers later.
3. **Why now**  
   Supply chain, power, and timing are gating the market. Delay is expensive.
4. **What the first engagement delivers**  
   Diligence, risk framing, pro forma, and a decision path.
5. **What to do next**  
   Provisional CTA: **Request a Project Readiness Review**.

### Current offer draft
The current draft offer is documented in `docs/phase-one-offer.md`.

- **Offer name:** `Konative Development Readiness Engagement`
- **Public CTA:** `Request a Project Readiness Review`
- **Commercial anchor:** internally anchored around `$300,000`, with public `engagement-based pricing`
- **Recommended duration:** `8-10 weeks`
- **Core output:** decision-grade diligence, pro forma, risk framing, and a go / no-go path

### Plain-language definitions for the team
- **Development quarterback:** the single lead partner coordinating site, power, cooling, supply chain, and execution decisions so the client does not manage fragmented specialists alone.
- **Out-of-ground execution:** the point where a project has moved beyond concept into a credible, decision-backed path toward physical development.
- **Decision-grade engagement:** work that produces enough diligence, risk framing, and financial clarity for a real go/no-go decision.

### Recommended public information architecture
- **Hero:** one-line promise + urgency-aware subhead + single primary CTA
- **Why Konative:** explain the market trap and why fragmented specialists fail
- **What We Do:** site, power, cooling, supply chain, pro forma, development orchestration
- **Who We Serve:** investment groups and Indigenous Development Corps
- **Why Now:** lead-time and supply-chain pressure as the forcing function
- **Engagement Model:** what phase-one work looks like and what artifacts clients receive
- **Trust Layer:** founder credentials, team bench, geography focus, anonymized proof if needed
- **CTA band:** repeated high-intent CTA
- **Footer:** legal/contact/brand structure clarity

### CTA fulfillment path for v1
- **Primary CTA mechanism:** simple consultation/pro-forma inquiry form, not a complex application workflow.
- **Minimum fields:** name, organization, email, project geography (`country/region or target site geography`), project type, timeline urgency, optional notes.
- **Routing:** submissions go directly to founder-owned email inbox and a simple v1 tracker. Default to founder inbox + spreadsheet if no CRM is selected yet.
- **Human SLA:** respond within 1 business day while deal volume is still founder-led.
- **Secondary option:** direct email link for high-intent introductions.
- **Do not add in v1:** calendar embed complexity, gated multi-step intake, or client portal login.

### Form resilience and abuse handling for v1
- Use a lightweight anti-spam measure such as a honeypot and/or trusted hosted form handling.
- Show clear success and error states; do not leave the user guessing whether the message was received.
- If email delivery fails or the form backend errors, the page should present a retry path and visible fallback contact method.
- Define `qualified inquiry` before launch so tracking is meaningful. Minimum definition: inquiry matches target buyer profile and includes a real project/timeline signal.

### Analytics minimum for v1
- Track at least these events from day one: primary CTA click, form submission success, and outbound contact-link click.
- Keep analytics lightweight and privacy-aware; if non-essential cookies are used, legal copy and consent behavior must reflect that.
- Store event definitions alongside launch notes so iteration is based on the same metrics everyone is reading.

### Messaging direction
Avoid generic phrases like "innovative infrastructure solutions." Lead with consequences:

- capital waiting
- land available
- timelines slipping
- complexity without a quarterback

The tone should feel sober, credible, and execution-minded, not futuristic or hype-driven.

### Trust layer fallback plan
If named case studies cannot be shown in the first release, trust should still be built through:

- founder and team credibility
- explicit articulation of the problem and decision process
- geography and domain specificity
- anonymized engagement patterns or sample deliverables
- clear Konative-branded contact/legal information

In other words, the first trust layer can be competence-first before it becomes proof-heavy.

### Legal and privacy for v1
- Keep the public site branded as **Konative only**.
- Publish privacy-policy/contact/legal pages required for a professional services site.
- Make form-copy expectations explicit: what information is collected, why it is collected, and how follow-up contact will occur.
- If analytics or marketing tools introduce non-essential cookies, add the appropriate consent flow instead of silently layering it in later.

## Distribution Plan
This deliverable is a public website, so distribution is straightforward:

- Launch on `konative.com` as the canonical public trust surface.
- Share directly in active deal conversations, proposals, and email intros.
- Use a lightweight deployment flow suitable for fast iteration, such as Vercel or equivalent.

- Validate redirect behavior, canonical routing, and metadata in staging before launch so the public experience is stable for both users and crawlers.
