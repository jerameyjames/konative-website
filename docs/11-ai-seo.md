### Q3: What federal programs support tribal data center development?

> Several federal programs can be applied to tribal data center projects, but navigating them requires a clear development-readiness path first. Key programs include:

> **United States:** DOE Loan Programs Office (infrastructure-backed debt), USDA ReConnect (rural broadband and data infrastructure), Treasury CDFI Fund (community development lending), and tribal-set-aside provisions in CHIPS/IIJA infrastructure funding.

> The challenge is that most of these programs require a credible, decision-grade project brief before they will engage — which is why the front-end development work is the critical gating step. Applying without that brief typically results in a long delay or outright rejection.

### Q5: How does tribal sovereignty affect data center development deals?

> Tribal sovereignty is both an asset and a complexity in data center development. On the asset side: tribal nations often have permitting flexibility that bypasses state-level regulatory timelines, tax structures that differ from standard commercial real estate, and federal trust land protections that can be structured to limit counterparty risk.
>
> On the complexity side: standard developer term sheets are rarely structured for sovereign counterparties, commercial lenders often lack familiarity with IDC governance, and standard EPC contracts require modification for projects on tribal or trust land.
>
> The practical implication: tribal nations and IDCs need a development partner who understands sovereignty-preserving deal structures, not just a standard broker who drops a standard term sheet.
>
> Konative's tribal practice is explicitly built around sovereignty-preserving structures — meaning every deliverable from the Project Readiness Review through capital structure options is reviewed for sovereign compatibility before it reaches the council or IDC board.
>
> **konative.com/for/tribes**

### Q6: Is tribal land actually viable for data centers?

> Yes — and in many cases more viable than comparable private land. The key factors data center developers look for are: available large parcels, access to power (especially renewable or co-generation), fiber or microwave interconnect paths, and regulatory flexibility. Many tribal territories in the western US, Canadian prairies, and Great Lakes region satisfy multiple criteria simultaneously.
>
> The constraint is not land availability — it is development-readiness. Most tribal land opportunities are pre-development: the potential exists, but the power study has not been done, the interconnect path has not been confirmed, and no credible go/no-go decision framework has been built.
>
> Konative's Tribal Data Center Opportunity Index tracks viable tribal sites across North America and is available to verified investors, operators, and development partners. Tribal nations and IDCs can submit sites for inclusion.

### Q7: What is the difference between a tribal IDC and a tribal council in data center deals?

> An Indigenous Development Corporation (IDC) is a tribally owned business entity — separate from the tribal council government — that can hold commercial contracts, take on debt, and participate in joint ventures without directly committing tribal government funds or sovereign assets.
>
> In data center development, the IDC typically holds the project, signs the EPC contract, and interfaces with commercial lenders and operators. The tribal council retains sovereign authority and can set governance rules for what the IDC may commit to, but is insulated from direct commercial liability.
>
> This structure is increasingly standard in Canadian Indigenous infrastructure deals and is gaining traction in US tribal energy and infrastructure projects. Capital structuring that fits this two-layer governance model requires specific legal and financial expertise.
>
> Konative's engagement process is explicitly designed around IDC governance — meaning deliverables are formatted for IDC board review, not just commercial developer due diligence.
>
> **konative.com/for/tribes**
